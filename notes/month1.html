Month 1 - reset phase.

This text comes in four sections.

1.  Learning how to Learn

This course focuses on trying to make you aware of how our brain works when
learning. The main objective of this is to try and exploit our 'brain power' so
that we can stop wasting our time trying to learn things in an old-fashioned
way. That, or simply to improve the way we navigate through the learning
process. A key concept about learning is that of 'chunking'. Chunking is
essentially learning something so well that it becomes second nature to you and
you stop thinking about 'how to do it' and you just do it. A chunk can be
thought of as a bundle of information inside our heads. The main objective when
it comes to learning is to be able to properly form chunks so that we can
gradually understand things in an intuitive manner without thinking about all of
the underlying details. Chunks can also work together so that you can group
several of them when trying to do something or learn something new.


Now, to be able to form chunks is another mammoth task. Chunking is best done by
properly taking the first steps of learning. These steps are: practice spaced
repetition; learn in small increments so that you don't overlearn; get some
exercise; use mnemonics; get a good night's sleep; stop procrastinating and use
something like the pomodoro technique; reward yourself periodically, and a few
more that I must've missed. In the end, even though we're all different and we
might have different approaches and preferences to learning, these tips and
techniques are tried and tested; and pretty much work for the majority.

Also, another great point about the course is to seek out an enriched
environment and take full advantage of its resources. When you place yourself
inside such an environment, you are usually exposed to much more information and
people from which you can learn.

2.  Code

So, this book is a 'recap of computers'. The mission of the book is to take some
cool, dense concepts and pack it into a very understandable formats. If you go
back to the very early days of telecommunications, you'll find that the presence
of binary code has always been strong. From binary onwards, it manages to build
up on the essential electrical knowledge needed to know how binary logic can be
represented in order to perform computations. During this 'electrical
foundation' period, you arrive at the relay, which is equivalent to the
transistor in terms of its switching capacity.

Moving past the electrical concepts, the next part tackles boolean logic and
building circuits based off this logic. It's a great way to illustrate the
operation of combinatorial circuits and it gives you the building blocks of an
ALU. This way, you can make a pretty smooth transition from digital logic to
computer architecture. And it's when you arrive at computer architecture when
the book really comes together. I feel that this is the goal of the book, to
organically arrive at architecture.

Moving on to the architecture part, you glue together all of the previous
concepts and end up with a clear picture of how the classic, seemingly immortal
Von Neumann architecture (It's kind of funny how Von Neumann just managed to
casually mold the future of computing while also being proficient at everything
he ever learned). Reaching this point is where the reader can finally be able to
understand how the classic computer chips worked; and since most mainstream
processors haven't really gone that far from this 'architecture norm', you're
able to really understand a lot of today's computing.

Finally having understood the huge hardware background of computers, you can
move on to the next big thin: Software and user interaction, which the book
manages to introduce at the very end. And here's where you see how programming
has come to be and how graphics where introduced into the very un-graphical
world of computing.

To be honest, the book really does mention a lot of concepts but in the end it
paints a pretty picture of what computers are and how our systems evolved to
eventually get to the world of computing. I still think it has way too many
concepts though.

3.  Make it Stick

This book and the coursera course complement each other very nicely. It goes on
and explains how to suck less at learning stuff and how to actually start
improving.

First off, managing your own time is key. I'm someone that has zero knowledge
about time management and I can attest the consequences it has. If you manage to
get past the hurdle of actually organizing your life (i.e. getting it together),
then you can go ahead and start practicing the outlined study techniques. And
these are really varied.

With studying techniques, you've got: spaced repetition; quizzing yourself on
stuff you don't know before you study it; studying in short intervals (here's
where those organization skills really come into play); quiz yourself on the
stuff you already studied; actually writing things down instead of typing them
into your computer; interleaving the topics you study; attending group study
sessions and so on.

Another great point which the book is the psychological one. If you don't have
the mindset necessary to persist on your learning, to overcome the difficulties
in your path and to try out new learning methods, then you're not really going
to get that far. That's just how reality works. Learning is just like any other
process and you need to iterate and check out what works out for you and what
just doesn't click with your style.

In the end, you just gotta work hard and clever.
