Week 3

Innovative thinking is something that's (at least in my opinion) been
historically rare. Even today, few people decide to go forth with
their own ideas that they themselves might consider a bit 'over the
top'. Mainly it's just because they don't feel that their idea will be
supported by others. Maybe they don't have the resources necessary to
materialize their idea. A lot of factors come into play

Most times, these crazy new ideas won't even appeal to the majority at
the beginning. It's only natural. These ideas are usually propagated
by people that have already tried them out and confirmed that they
yield some positive result. Like any social change, it is usually
brought to the table by a select few that follow the idea with a
passion and eventually, if the community deems it to be worthy, the
idea becomes something mainstream. 

A great example of how this slow, societal change progresses can be
seen pretty much every day in the tech industry. When new projects
come out, those who were there from the beginning will stick to it
passionately. Then, if these passionate 'evangelists' manage to
actually prove that their project is beneficial to some process or
community, then most likely that project will gain more
traction. After the community grows large enough, these great ideas
may even become de facto standards (like perhaps what git has become
inside the world of version control, or what TDD and BDD have become
in modern software development). Ideas that seem good need to be tried
out to see what happens. You never know how impactful an idea can
become (like the role of quantum computing in the future).

Even though all this talk of great ideas is nice and poetic, many
times ideas are backed up because of the people that defend them and
not the ideas themselves (for example, Windows is very widely used due
to Microsoft's business tactics).

But, it's when you realize that the impact you yourself may have on
others when presenting new ideas, the more social the process
becomes. Having a great idea is one thing, but the next steps are
usually easier if you present yourself as an approachable, positive
person (although this isn't necessarily a rule, it usually helps out a
lot).

So, if you can be a team player and are also able to polish out good
ideas, that's already a great start for any new project that you might
want to present to the community. It's also the most organic witness
the life cycle of an idea. Many times this organic approach is the
complete opposite to what big corporations do (for example intel
selling the same architecture with new features to the point that we
still have remains of it inside our own processors). But not
everything's usually that easy. You need to put in a lot of effort
into your idea in order to get some kind of functional 'prototype' up
and running. Here's where the 'skill' factor comes into play: In order
to flesh out a cool idea (let's say a cool text editor) you'd probably
have to start with the basics, and that is, programming the
editor. Many times our imagination is limited due to the fact that a
lot of technology lacks the expressivity necessary to bring ideas to
life in a very short period of time. There's usually a lot of leg work
involved so, perhaps a good idea can be creating new, specialized and
very expressive tools for different fields. That way, we can start
bridging the gap between ourselves and computers.

The tech industry obviously excels at pushing ideas onto consumers,
and it shows. Nowadays, we are constantly bombarded by ads, videos,
articles, or any piece of media that made it's way to us thanks to a
myriad of data analysis tools. We are the data being loaded onto the
machine. Thanks to this, we can live the effects of what big data (on
the societal level) really means. But that's not the end of the
story. Mass data analysis can also be used in very helpful ways, such
as fraud detection or aiding the healthcare industry. Everything's not
that bad.

In the end, ideas are just ideas and we need to work on them and
present them to the world to see if they can stand the test of time to
prove their worth. The best ideas are those that can be challenged
over and over again and still stand afterwards. I don't think that an
idea can last forever but, usually those that stick around are pretty
nice.
