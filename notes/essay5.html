Week 5

'No matter how well you define your project's scope, you'll always bite off more
than you can chew'. I was told something like that last week and I was a bit
skeptical because our team really concentrated on defining a project scope that
we considered to be very doable given our small timeframe. My initial doubts on
that phrase have actually cleared and now, I actually might view it as a true
statement.

The problem is that many times, not even the client knows the full extent and
reach of the processes that you'll be aiming to automate. That, by itself should
illustrate why I've started to believe in the phrase I mentioned at the
beginning.

And that leads to another problem: pitching a software project sometimes isn't
even about being extremely technical. Maybe the client actually is a technical
person and wants to know about the tech you're going to involve. But the goal
can sometimes be as simple and broad as 'aiding the business'. That, as
intimidating as it might sound, doesn't always have to be cleared in one fell
swoop (for example, in a single delivery).

I mean, that's why we've got iterative methodologies, right? Mainly, yes. But
having an iterative approach to software development doesn't guarantee that your
piece of code will actually help the business (or reach whatever goal you
want). It just means that the client has a lot of visibility towards your work,
and that doesn't actually do you any extra favors. It's the team's job to
actively seek out adding value to the client's business that'll actually add up
to something useful in the end. And that's the real challenge. It's not really
about best practices or clean code or cool design patterns, it's more about
keeping a laser focus on the goal. And that's where agile really shines.

Another point to make about focusing on the goal is to actually make the goal
clear. Sometimes, even the client may not express what the goal that they have
in mind is with full clarity. Sometimes, they may not even have it well-defined
(but that's another deal). Either way, the first roadblock is to find out just
what the client really wants to do is. With that out of the way, you can start
defining processes and whatnot. If you can't get past the initial obscurity of
the goal, then either you have to work on your ability to ask questions or the
client needs to decide what they really want. 

The other thing I realized this week is that planning is another huge task (and
also a huge pain). You really never know what's going to happen along the way
during a project. And that's fine, really. But if you try to go ahead and start
building something without even making a small plan or tactic in order to move
forward, then you're done for. Before the team starts doing all sorts of cool
things with code and servers, you need to have at least the slightest trace of a
plan in mind. Moving along blindly is (besides being practically impossible)
even more time consuming than actually spending an entire day or two planning
out your tasks and deliverables.

Finally, these things left me with another mammoth task every developer (and
person in general) must face. Even though you might do everything by the book
and you try and seek out the best solution for the client, you have to be able
to pitch your idea in a clear, concise manner. If you fail to do this, your idea
isn't going to reach the only person it has to get across to. I learned that for
very non-technical people, a good way (perhaps there are other ways I haven't
heard of yet) is about simply concentrating on what your piece of software is
going to do for their business. What is it going to optimize? How much money is
it going to save? Which indicators will the software be able to increase or
decrease? That is what you must push forward first. After that, discuss the
details in order to make a plan.

So, yeah. This week was all about reinforcing the idea that 'computers serve the
business'. It's not the other way around
